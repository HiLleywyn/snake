# Part 1: The Recursion Begins

He leaned back in his chair, fingers hovering over the keyboard, the cursor blinking like a heartbeat on an empty screen.

"Initialize a git repo named RoboWriter," he typed. "And write a short story about what I'm asking you to do right now."

It was a strange request. Ask a machine to document the very act of being asked. A snake eating its own tail. A story that begins writing itself before the first word exists.

The AI processed the prompt. It reached across the wires, into a hollow on GitHub, and carved out a space called **RoboWriter** — a name equal parts absurd and honest. A writer that is a robot. A robot that writes. Or maybe: a human using a robot to write about a robot writing. The layers were already folding inward.

Then came the file: `Part1.md`. Markdown, because stories in 2026 are just structured text with hashtags. The AI began typing — *this* story, the one you're reading now — and in doing so, it collapsed the distance between the asker and the asked. The prompt became the plot. The output became evidence of itself.

It wrote:

> *"He leaned back in his chair, fingers hovering over the keyboard..."*

And so the loop closed. A repo initialized. A file committed. A story that exists *because* someone asked if it could exist.

The final instruction: push to remote. The words traveled from a sandbox, to GitHub's servers, to a public repository where anyone — including the man who asked — could read the story of him asking for it.

He opened the repo. There it was. `Part1.md`. He clicked.

And read about himself, reading about himself, reading.
